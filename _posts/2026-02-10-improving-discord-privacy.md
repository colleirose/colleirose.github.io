Especially in light of Discord's new demands for ID verification, I've seen various discussions about Discord's privacy practices online and I feel that pointing all of this out now is important given how much privacy advice seems to consist of people repeating things they heard before without considering what they're saying.

As you are probably aware by now, Discord is going to be requiring ID verification for some features. If you want to speak in Discord stage channels, view NSFW content, or disable the annoying message filter that sends mesages from new contacts to a separate inbox, you have to give Discord either an ID or a selfie. Discord's only explanation for why this will be secure after their previous third-party vendor got hacked and 70,000 records were exposed is that they are now giving even more third-party vendors access to the same information while terminating their contract with the one that was hacked previously. To my understanding, there has been no explanation of why these new vendors are more secure or even who they are. Also, as most people are aware by now, like usual, they quickly backtracked on most of the privacy claims and also announced an "experiment" for UK users where they <i>will</i> store your ID; I am sure it is a coincidence that ID verification was also initially forced only on UK users. This company has a long history of telling half-truths and outright lying, so you should not trust any of their privacy promises.

Some people seem to think this will only be a problem for people viewing NSFW channels. I'm not going to go on about the argument for privacy in general right now, but as for Discord specifically, the fact that Discord has determined that things like speaking in a stage channel or disabling the annoying "message requests" feature (which effectively removes pings for new DMs and does nothing else useful) are both inappropriate for minors suggests they don't seem to be thinking very hard about what they will classify as NSFW. Also, most people who have used Discord long enough are familiar with the company's inability to make any reasonable moderation decisions, and I also question why we should trust that Discord won't bend to pressure from regulators or shareholders to, for example, declare any LGBTQ content as "obscene" and "perverted" and therefore cut off minors from SFW support resources, <a href="https://www.erininthemorning.com/p/senator-admits-kids-online-safety">which is what you see when you look deeper into most of the "save the children" demands</a>. Discord's classification of content borders on being as reliable as a coin toss. It's very important to discuss exactly what data Discord is collecting and what you can do about it.

# Data collected by Discord

## Actual new developments
An interesting detail that hasn't been discussed enough is <a href="https://archive.is/PvpAx">one particular statement</a> that has since been removed from The Verge's article about this:
<blockquote>
Discord is also rolling out an age inference model that analyzes metadata like the types of games a user plays, their activity on Discord, and behavioral signals like signs of working hours or the amount of time they spend on Discord.
</blockquote>

After publishing the article, The Verge issued this correction:
<blockquote>
“For most adults, age verification won’t be required, as Discord’s age inference model uses account information such as account tenure, device and activity data, and aggregated, high-level patterns across Discord communities. Discord does not use private messages or any message content in this process,” Savannah Badalich, Discord’s global head of product policy, tells <i>The Verge.</i>
</blockquote>

Neither statements are particularly good, but there has since been no confirmation of whether "the types of games a user plays" will still be analyzed. That probably sounds like the least important part of what Discord is collecting, but the interesting aspect here is how Discord detects "the types of a games a user plays". As most people are aware, when you use Discord's desktop client and open a game, Discord automatically detects that the game is open and updates your activity status, unless you specifically disable this feature under the "Activity Privacy" section.

Discord does this by monitoring what processes are open on your system, but they have generally claimed that this data is only processed on the client side and the server only receives information about what games are detected if you enable it. So far, there has been no reason to doubt this. However, for Discord to analyze "the types of games a user plays", they would have to be storing activity status information long-term. Therefore, in a best case scenario, Discord is keeping a log of each time you open a game that triggers the activity status feature, when you opened it, how long you played it, etc. Combining this with the fact that it's fairly common to set activity statuses for things other than games (e.g., the VSCode activity status extension), anyone who uses the ordinary desktop app will likely have a long pattern of their activity logged. Although this isn't personally identifiable per se, it's still obviously rather creepy, and while it's not the most important aspect of their data collection, I think it's important to mention here. 

Furthermore, considering how difficult it is to reverse engineer the Discord client each time a new update is released to determine what exactly is being collected, and that Discord currently seems to hold the position that the only type of information they aren't allowed to analyze as they please is private DMs, I would be highly suspicious of this statement.

## Important and already known facts that do not get much attention

Besides this, everything else can mostly be interpreted as confirmation that Discord is just as creepy as anyone who has bothered to look at their data export and the company's privacy policy would already believe it to be. Discord collects detailed logs of when you initiate various actions like sending a message or opening a channel, and as a result, metadata on deleted DMs will persist even after deletion. Furthermore, from some past investigation and experimentation, I speculate that Discord persists some types of deleted messages. 

<i>Firstly</i>, to quote <a href="https://archive.is/Clqrr">this article on the official Discord support site</a> which is linked to from <a href="https://archive.ph/uSx5R">the Discord privacy policy</a> (emphasis added):
<blockquote>
Deleted content will also be deleted from Discord’s systems, but we may retain content longer if we have a legal obligation to preserve it as described below.  <b>Public posts may also be retained for 180 days to two years for use by Discord</b> as described in our Privacy Policy (for example, to help us train models that proactively detect content that violates our policies).
</blockquote>

Notably, despite what the page claims, the actual usage here isn't really described that much in the privacy policy. The only mention of the retention of "public posts" is in this article that the privacy policy links to. Furthermore, there is still <u>no official confirmation of what constitutes a "public post"</u>, but I have one theory. 

From some past testing, I'm aware of at least two incidents that would suggest <u>enabling the "community server" feature is likely the criteria to be considered "public"</u>:
* I was an admin in a private, friends-only server that had <u>enabled the community server feature</u>. This server had about fifty members. A year or two after it was created, several of the channels were deleted, and then some time later the entire server was deleted. For unrelated reasons, I had requested my Discord data export I believe a few months or so after it was deleted (though it could have been a different amount of time), and noticed several messages from that deleted server and its deleted channels in my Discord data export.
* Once, someone joined a random public <u>server discovery</u> server (note: being a community server is a prerequisite to being in server discovery) and spammed various troll messages. One of these was "I want an 8 year old wife like Allah intended". I responded to this by stating "great idea", then some time later I deleted my message and reported the troll to Discord through the in-app reporting flow. However, a day ater I sent the report, Discord sent me a violation the next day for "child safety", citing the then-deleted "great idea" message, <u>and</u> sent me an email around the same time saying they took action on my report of another user and thanked me for "keeping Discord safe", etc. This server was incredibly dead at the time and so it's less likely that anyone else was looking at messages and decided to report them, though I can't prove it.

This could probably be tested further with some more controlled experiments with multiple accounts and changing settings in servers, but I just don't have the time for this and am mostly satisfied with my hypothesis. However, <u>I have never been able to find deleted DMs in my data export, nor find any other proof that Discord records them.</u> Of course, absence of evidence isn't evidence of abscence, and of course, if you are concerned about law enforcement surveillance, a preservation order would require Discord to retain DMs anyway. However, I find it notable that there has yet to be any evidence of Discord preserving DMs.

Another important thing to note is that Discord exposes a lot of metadata to users that can be accessed with a modded client, but this isn't clearly disclosed to users. Some examples of this include the exact timestamp of when a user account or server is created, the owner of a server regardless of whether they have the owner crown symbol, whether a user is timed out in a server and for how long, whether invites are paused in a server, various details about channels you don't have access to (name, creation date, last message date, description, and all permissions for channel access), the names and permissions of all roles in a server and the time they were created, every channel-specific permission override, etc. Furthermore, the "mutual friends" feature on Discord can't be turned off, so someone with a lot of free time can confirm/deny whether someone is on your friends list just by friending that person (eg by pretending to be someone else, or just hoping the person accepts random friend requests).

# What to do

A lot of people say that Discord's tracking is unavoidable. While I dislike Discord for a lot of reasons, I don't agree. There is no way to make Discord not record data, but there are numerous ways to reduce what data it can record to begin with. Furthermore, as a practical matter, Discord is not going to be able to retain everything that ever happens on the platform forever (it is too expensive to pay for the storage space for one). Therefore, while it is a lot easier to preserve your privacy by just not using Discord, it isn't black-and-white; you can still attain a reasonable amount of privacy if you're careful.

First, some important strategies to note regarding deleting information from Discord if you don't have GDPR protections:
* One, Discord doesn't officially let you mass-delete your messages, but in my experience, the <a href="https://victornpb.github.io/undiscord/">Undiscord</a> tool is very effective, though there is a risk when using it because it violates the ToS by automating account actions (i.e., "self-botting"). Additionally, if you email privacy@discord.com with a channel and message ID for a message you sent but can't access, they can delete it for you, but they won't let you mass-delete messages. You can obtain these IDs in your Discord data export.
* Two, if someone posts personal information about you without your consent on Discord and you want to reduce your exposure, use the in-app reporting flow (though you really don't want to get to that point anyway). 
* Three, unless you don't want to make an alt, I don't recommend using the "delete account" button. Instead, on the account you want to delete after you've deleted its messages (account deletion doesn't delete messages, it just assigns them to a "Deleted User" account), send a message saying something like "I'm 12 years old" (or any age below 13), and then use an alt to report this message. Preferably do this in DMs or a small server where people won't immediately respond to say you're lying, or would delete the message and ban you. Discord is legally required to obtain parental consent to collect the personal information of users under 13, and Discord does not want to deal with doing that, so they ban anyone who claims to be under 13. After the time to verify your ID to prove you're 13+ lapses (yes, Discord has had ID verification for this for a long time, but people rarely encounter it), your account will be permanently deleted. If Discord retains information on people they believe to be under 13, they can be subject to significant legal penalties, so I would argue that this is probably the best you can do.

As for using Discord itself, there are various things you need to do here.

<i>Firstly</i>, during sign-up, unless you're fine with Discord knowing your approximate location (at around the city level or so) and Internet service provider, you should use a VPN or Tor to hide your IP address. (Also note that if your threat model includes law enforcement, you MUST hide your IP address; your ISP and therefore the police through a court order can identify who is using some IP address at a given time.) If you use a VPN, use a very popular one and do not use any kind of "dedicated IP" feature. The primary benefit of a VPN is that you will be using the same IP address as thousands of other people at the same time, which makes it very difficult for a VPN company to trace you even if they were ordered to. If you can spare 5 euros per month, I would recommend buying a subscription to Mullvad VPN. 

I strongly encourage you to use a VPN kill switch feature on whatever device you use Discord on as you're otherwise very likely to accidentally open Discord without your VPN at some point due to the app often running in the background or being used frequently. (If you've bothered to read this far, you probably intend to use Discord more than a few times, yes?) I also recommend routinely exporting your Discord data export (the feature is available in Settings > Data & Privacy) and using a text editor to search through the files to make sure you don't see your real IP address or any other information inadvertently exposed.

To my understanding, using Tor will likely lead to Discord forcing you into phone verification, which you can bypass with <a href="https://sms24.me">sms24.me</a>, but it is <u>very</u> unreliable and annoying to do so, not to mention the fact that sms24.me is full of ads and trackers, sometimes stops receiving messages, and randomly breaks with ad blockers. In the past, using a VPN would also force phone verification, but as far as I can tell, this is no longer the case so long as you verify your email in a reasonable time after signing up (generally prefer to do that as soon as possible) and interact with the account somewhat. Typically, after creating a new account, I will immediately click through the various tutorial popups, create a server and send a message in it, then verify my email and then set up my user profile. I've never been forced into phone verification when doing this.

Any email address you use to sign up should ideally be one that isn't associated with any of your real-life details. I use a ProtonMail account for everything that I don't intend to tie to my real-life identity. ProtonMail <u>does not encrypt communications with people who aren't also using the service</u>, but it has significantly less automatic tracking than Gmail and other common providers.

<i>Secondly</i>, this one is very obvious, but in Settings > Data & Privacy, disable everything under "How Discord uses your data", and in Settings > Activity Privacy, disable "share my activity" (unless you want people to see your activity status for whatever reason).

<i>Thirdly</i>, when using Discord, you should use either the web app, mobile app, or if you're on Linux, <a href="https://flathub.org/en/apps/com.discordapp.Discord">the Flatpak version of the desktop app</a>. All of these will make Discord sandboxed from the rest of your system so that it can't monitor the running processes without your permission.

If you use the mobile app on Android, unless you insist on having to use the modern UI for Discord, install <a href="https://github.com/Aliucord/Aliucord">Aliucord</a>, which blocks trackers and crash reporting (it isn't possible to block tracking of things like when you load a channel or send a message because those actions have to contact the server, but it prevents sending crash reports and prevents various analytics events for actions that don't require sending data to the server). If you use the desktop app on Linux, Windows, or macOS, install <a href="https://vencord.dev/">Vencord</a>, a Discord client mod that also contains tracker blocking, similarly to Aliucord. 

Vencord also offers several plugins to view various hidden details as I discussed in the previous section, so I recommend checking those plugins out to get an idea of what information is exposed on Discord. Do note that on desktop, Discord updates may inadvertently remove Vencord due to the updates overwriting Discord files, but you can safely reinstall Vencord without any issue. Also, some Discord updates will change parts of Discord's inner workings, so after a new update, some Vencord plugins may not work and you'll need to wait for a Vencord update.

If you use the Flatpak version of the desktop app, the app will not be able to view running processes on your system, and it will be fully supported by Vencord. However, you can further improve this by installing <a href="https://flathub.org/en/apps/com.github.tchx84.Flatseal">Flatseal</a> and removing privileges from the Discord app. Firstly, you can disable the "Smart cards" permission (`socket=pcsc`) without any clear issues, though I suspect this might break using security keys for 2FA and login. You can disable the "All devices" permission (`device=all`) to prevent Discord from accessing your camera, microphone, or USB devices (which of course breaks talking in calls and likely also breaks using security keys), but if you do so, you need to re-enable the "GPU acceleration" permission (`device=dri`) that would otherwise be granted by "All devices", because if you don't, the app can't open.

You can also disable most file system permissions to prevent Discord from viewing your files without permission (surprisingly, this doesn't yet seem to create any problems because Discord can still spawn the normal file picker for downloading and uploading files, though dragging-and-dropping files doesn't work), but you must leave `xdg-run/speech-dispatcher` and `xdg-run/pipewire-0` enabled for accessibility support and screensharing support respectively (screen sharing requires you to manually approve a prompt to share your screen, Discord can't just start screensharing on its own). Note that Vencord will add `/home/[your username]/.config/Vencord/dist` to the allowed file paths, and this must be kept for Vencord to work correctly. Vencord also addds `host-etc:ro` to the allowed paths, but as far as I can tell, there aren't any side effects to removing it on my Arch Linux system, though your experience may vary.

<i>Fourthly</i>, and also rather obvious, don't use Discord for private communications if you can help it. I'm aware of there being a handful of encryption tools for Discord, but these aren't audited or developed to the same standard as major messaging apps like SimpleX (if you need both private and anonymous messages and can sacrifice usability) or Signal (if you need private messages with worse anonymity but better usability). Additionally, some of them seem to automatically send the encrypted messages on your behalf, which could constitute self-botting and risk an account suspension. These also rely on trusting Discord to not deliberately manipulate messages to perform a man-in-the-middle attack during key exchange, although I would argue this risk is more theoretical as there isn't any current legal authority to force Discord to do this and Discord would experience a massive loss of business if they were caught voluntarily tampering with communications.

# Some takeaways

Discord is a rather frustrating service, and I think anyone who has used it for a while now understands these problems. Regardless, a lot of people still use this website for one reason or another. Many private/secure/decentralized messaging apps have some significant UX problems and I don't really expect Discord to die off any time soon, so it's best to try to reduce harm rather than place unreasonable expectations on users to just abandon every proprietary app overnight.

I'm hoping that the actions people are taking to protest Discord's ID verification mandate will lead to Discord reconsidering their terrible decisions, but I'm not too hopeful. The company has convinced itself that it's allowed to randomly backtrack on every promise about what data is collected and used for, and I don't think anyone can really expect this company to get better. All of these changes are being done so that Discord can prepare for its IPO, and when that happens the site will probably just be filled with even more ads and garbage. Regardless, I'm still going to be using Discord for now and trying to work around its problems. If you also intend to keep using Discord, I hope that you'll take this privacy advice into consideration.
